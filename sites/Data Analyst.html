Data Analyst




WHAT DO DATA ANALYSTS DO?


Data analysts translate numbers into plain English Every business collects data, whether it's sales figures, market research, logistics, or transportation costs.
A data analyst's job is to take that data and use it to help companies make better business decisions.
This could mean figuring out how to price new materials for the market, how to reduce transportation costs, solve issues that cost the company money, or determine how many people should be working on Saturdays.
There are many different types of data analysts in the field, including operations analysts, marketing analysts, financial analysts, etc.


HOW MUCH DO DATA ANALYSTS MAKE?


This depends on experience and what type of data analyst you are.
According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) financial analysts made a median hourly rate of $35.75, or $74,350 annually in 2010.
Market research analysts made $29.12 an hour, or $60,570 annually, and operations research analysts made $34.12 per hour, or $$70,960 annually.


WHAT ARE THE EDUCATION REQUIREMENTS?


A bachelor's degree is needed for most entry-level jobs, and a master's degree will be needed for many upper-level jobs.
Most analysts will have degrees in fields like math, statistics, computer science, or something closely related to their field.
Strong math and analysis skills are needed.
Depending on the field you go into, certification is available.
JOB SKILLS AND REQUIREMENTS Analytical Skills: Data analysts work with large amounts of data: facts, figures, and number crunching.
You will need to see through the data and analyze it to find conclusions.
Communication Skills: Data analysts are often called to present their findings, or translate the data into an understandable document.
You will need to write and speak clearly, easily communicating complex ideas.
Critical Thinking: Data analysts must look at the numbers, trends, and data and come to new conclusions based on the findings.
Attention to Detail: Data is precise.
Data analysts have to make sure they are vigilant in their analysis to come to correct conclusions.
Math Skills: Data analysts need math skills to estimate numerical data.

Exploratory Analysis: Exploratory data analysis attempts to find relationships, discoveries, links, and measurements from various measurements to create specific ideas and hypotheses.
An example of exploratory analysis is the work of a group of amateurs who analyzed many space data collected by Kepler's telescope and found a solar system of four planets by analyzing the properties of light.
CONCLUSION: One of the most common data analyzes in scientific research, goes beyond exploratory analysis to see if the patterns detected are valid to be behind the totals of available data.
An example is the relationship between environmental pollution and life expectancy at the state level in the United States.
This analysis measures and calculates the different relationships between available measurements.
Predictive Analysis: While the former type measures relations and computes their values, predictive analysis predicts certain measurements from existing measurements.
For example, what statisticians do in predicting the outcome of an election by analyzing the predictive behavior observed in the questionnaires.
Causal Analysis: This analysis calculates certain measures if other measures change, for example, calculating the effect of a particular medical practice on reducing a particular disease.
Mechanical analysis: Previous causal analysis creates a relationship with a certain percentage of the occurrence and the impact of data may be very huge, for example, for decades, data say that smoking leads to cancer, but it is not sure you may not die cancer despite smoking.
What mechanical analysis does is to establish a definite and inevitable relationship between two measurements.
Objectives [edit] Data analysis aims to prepare a so-called data model system.
This is a major activity of the analysis phase and data is often modeled using graphical models, ie diagrams and drawings that are somewhat similar to data flow charts.
Data modeling steps [edit] As with modeling operations and preparing data flow charts, data modeling is usually done in three steps.
The first instance of data analysis takes place at the system analysis stage, while steps 2 and 3 are done at the design stage.
[Edit] Preparing a conceptual model of data [edit] This step is also called system data modeling.
A model that reflects the main topics (objects) of the data and their relationships with each other is constructed.
Analysis at this level is called analysis of content or meaning.
Analysis of relationships [edit] The conceptual model is improved by redesigning the entities in a way that minimizes duplicates and turns the entities into simplified relationships that can be handled flexibly and easily.
This process is also called normalization or normalization of data and the construction of a relational model.
